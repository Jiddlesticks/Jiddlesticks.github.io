Getting Started

Introduction

The Chemistry Lab at Nucor Steel Seattle works directly with the Melt Shop, engaging in tasks ranging from analyzing steel samples to organizing and assigning finished products. Our primary goal is to ensure that every batch (known as a "heat") of steel not only aligns with Nucor's internal quality standards but also meets all ASTM (American Society for Testing and Materials) specifications.

The responsibilities of a Chemistry Lab Technician include:

  • Preparation and analysis of steel samples on optical emission spectrometers and other testing equipment.
  • Communication of grade specifications and chemistry aims to furnace and caster crews.
  • Coordination of Melt Shop, Caster Runout, and Billet Crane operations.
  • Organization and fulfillment of Melt Shop and Rolling Mill work orders using a variety of mill productivity programs.
  • Housekeeping and general maintenance of lab equipment.

The purpose of this document is to offer basic information and convenient references for new teammates undergoing training for this position. It is not intended as a substitute for any Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs), Safe Job Procedures (SJPs), or hands on training.

Safety

Safety is our top priority at Nucor. Basic knowledge of proper Personal Protective Equipment (PPE), Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) procedures, observations, and reporting policies is essential for safely performing tasks in the lab.

While the chemistry lab may not be as hazardous as other areas of the mill, there are still a number of precautions that need to be taken:

  • Alley Traffic: Exercise caution while entering and exiting the lab; forklifts and other equipment are frequently used in this area.
  • Hot Samples: Always assume that samples (steel and slag) are hot; never touch them barehanded.
  • Retrieving Samples: When retrieving samples in the alley, pull the bin out of the housing to prevent a dropped sample from contacting your hand.
  • Grinding Safety: Exercise caution while grinding samples, watching out for pinch points that could catch your glove and lead to contact with the grinding surface. When possible, use the auto-grinder or milling machine, as these are hands-free devices.
  • Equipment Maintenance: Never perform equipment maintenance without first confirming if a lockout is required.

Melt Shop Process

In the Melt Shop, steel production begins in the scrapyard. Crane operators load scrap into charge buckets, which are then moved into the shop. The charge crane then picks the bucket up and drops the scrap into the furnace (known as charging the furnace). It takes three charges to make a heat, and about fifty minutes to melt the scrap and get it to 3100 °F, which is the ideal tapping Tapping a heat is pouring the steel through a small opening on the underside of the furnace (called a taphole) into a ladle. temperature. Before the heat is tapped into the ladle, a sample (FR sample) is taken through the door of the furnace and sent for analysis. The Melter adds alloysTechnically a misnomer; this is what we call alloying elements. Alloying elements such as carbon, manganese, and vanadium are added to steel to strengthen it. for the desired grade, and the process repeats.

Meanwhile, the ladle is moved to the stir station using the ladle fork. A stir rod is dipped into the steel and begins blowing nitrogen into the ladle to agitate the steel and incorporate all of the additions from the Melter. After about five minutes of stirring, another sample (called a ladle top, or LT 1 sample) is sent for analysis. LT 1 helps verify the incorporation of alloys and assists in determining if further additions are needed. Most of the time, a second ladle top (LT 2) sample is taken to confirm alloy mixing and assess the need for additional adjustments.

The ladle, now well-stirred and at the correct temperature to cast (~2900 °F), is picked up by another crane and transported to the caster.

At the caster, the ladle is set on a turret The ladle turret is a rotating platform on top of the caster that allows two ladles to be set on top of the caster. This makes it possible to have one heat pouring and another staged to pour next. , opened from the bottom, and poured into the tundish A tundish is a refractory-lined container used in continuous casting. It serves as an intermediary vessel between the ladle and the molds. The tundish helps regulate the flow of molten steel into the molds, ensuring a consistent and controlled distribution of steel to create billets. through a nozzle. The tundish acts as a funnel, draining through nozzles to create four strands that pour into molds. A sample is taken from the first half of the heat pouring (called a tundish sample, or TN 1) via one of those strands to verify that the chemistry meets grade. Another sample is taken from the second half of the heat (TN 2) to ensure the heat's homogeneity. The final chemistry (FI) for the heat is determined by averaging TN 1 and TN 2.

As the billets cool and are cut to length, they continue down a roller conveyor (called the runout), where they are weighed, counted, marked, and pushed onto the runout table. Finally, a billet crane picks up the cooled billets and stacks them in rolling order for future processing.

Each heat will have at least 4 samples, but the norm is 6. The order of samples, as explained above, is FR→LT1→LT2 (if necessary) →TN1→TN2. When TN2 is sent down, instead of running it individually, it is averaged with TN1 to make up the final, or FI analysis. Occasionally, if there are issues stirring or concern that a heat won't make grade, a sample can be taken from the ladle stream as soon as it opens; this is called a ladle bottom, or LB. Since the sample is taken before the steel makes it to the tundish, it allows up to 10 minutes of extra time before billets begin cutting to determine if the heat needs to be diverted. When transitioning between grades, billet samples are taken from the front or end of a heat to ensure no incompatible chemistries are mixing. FR and LT samples are sent down using the pneumatic tube system in the lab; LB and TN samples are dropped down a pipe in the alley just outside of the lab.

During normal operations, we can be running samples from up to three heats at a time, so keeping track of where you are in the process with each heat is extremely important.

QMOS

QMOS (Quad Mill Operating System), also known as Quad, is a central tool in our daily operations in the chemistry lab. Quad Infotech's description of the software is:

"QMOS enables users to schedule the production of the steel plants in the Melt Shop and the Rolling Mill while optimizing the process and yield. The schedule, along with real time production and quality information, is disseminated to the steel mill operators and managers empowering them to take immediate and informed actions if and when necessary.

QMOS manages the entire process from receiving customer orders, creation of production schedule for each work center, as well as managing the demand for the steel. The coverage continues all the way to the production of the billets and further down to packaging and shipment of semi-finished and finished products."

QMOS has modules for nearly every mill process, accessible through various shortcuts. Each shortcut opens a program that contains all of the necessary modules for your position.

The programs utilized in the lab are:

  • Melt Shop Production [MSP]: Used to schedule and track all processes (chemistries, scheduling, billet counts, etc.) in the Melt Shop.
  • Production Scheduling and Control [PSC]: Used to track billet handling/stacking as well as Rolling Mill work orders and production.
  • Data: Used to look up data, specifications, and metrics used throughout the mill. In the lab, we primarily use it to look up specifications for each grade of steel that we produce.
  • Billet Yard: Shows a live display of all of the heats in the billet yard, which are organized into stacks.

Chemistry Aims & Specs

Each grade of steel that we produce has a specific set of internal and external chemistry specifications. Our internal specs change depending on the residual elements Residual elements are the elements in the scrap that can't be changed in a meaningful way (oxidized out or added). The residual elements are P, Cu, Cr, Mo, Sn, and Ni. in the scrap. In general, higher levels of residuals, such as chromium, enhance the steel's strength, reducing the need for additional alloying elements.

We can access the specs from the data module. Make sure that 'Product Definition' is selected in the left column, then double click the 'Spec Lookup' button, which is the last option in the right column.

Once 'Spec Lookup' has been opened, you will see all of our grades on the left side of the screen. When a grade is selected, it shows the chemistry ranges and aims on the right side of the screen. Each grade has multiple sets of aims depending on the Cr level. When the Cr level of a heat is determined, select the range that is appropriate, and the proper spec will appear below.

Each Cr range tends to be similar, but going up or down a range normally changes the Mn and V. This varies from grade to grade, so be sure to check on every heat.

The main columns on the chemistry section of this screen have specific meanings that must be understood to ensure the accuracy of our chemistries and the quality of each heat.

  • Min / Max: These represent our absolute ranges, some of which are set by ASTM, CSA, or customers. If we go above or below these numbers, QMOS will automatically put the heat on hold. More often than not, the grade will need to be changed to something compatible with the chemistry (this is called a diversion). Heats that won't pass for the intended grade can be regraded to something else. Generally this means that the billet length and stacking area will have to be changed as well.
  • Int Min / Int Max: The internal min and max. This is the ideal chemistry range. You'll notice that there are a few points A point is the term we use for a single decimal point change on an element. One point of carbon added to a heat with a .43 would make it .44; a ten point decrease from a .043 sulfur would be .033; etc. between this range and the absolute min and max values; depending on the residuals, heats will sometimes pass with those values, but should be approved by **** or **** until you are more experienced.
  • Aim: These are the values that we communicate to the furnace and caster crews when making additions to the heat.

Navigating OXSAS

The program we use to control the spectrometers (4460 and 3460) is called OXSAS. Here's a description directly from ThermoFisher Scientific:

"Thermo Scientific OXSAS software for OES instruments runs a variety of analyses from simple tasks to complex jobs, including the development of new analytical methods and calibrations as well as rapid high-quality routine elemental analysis. OES provides rapid analysis of carbon and low nitrogen along with all other alloying elements in iron and steel..."

The 'Quantitative Analysis' window in OXSAS is used to analyze and display the full chemistry of a sample. The screen is divided into 29 columns, each displaying either an element's value or a calculated value. It's important to note that there are a few unnecessary calculated values on this screen, which are holdovers from grades we no longer produce. These include all of the columns beginning with a Y, T, or an E.

In the 'Quantitative Analysis' window of OXSAS, each row represents a single burn of a sample. These rows can be selected or deselected based on the quality of the burn and are then averaged out in the bottom row. The average of these samples is what we save to QMOS and communicate to the crew. Ensuring the quality of each burn within an analysis is crucial, and it's equally important that the rows contributing to the average are similar to one another. This practice ensures that we report accurate numbers to the crew, and ultimately the customer.

Although all of the numbers on this screen are important, the crew is only concerned with the elements that can be directly controlled by our processes. This can be used as a cheat sheet but should not be used in place of the specsheet.

The other windows within OXSAS that we frequently use are:

  • Batches: Used to select batches of samples that will be used for a drift correction. The purpose of drift correction is to maintain the instrument's measurement accuracy over time by adjusting for changes in its performance. Think of it as a tune-up for consistent and reliable analysis. A link to the procedure is provided at the end of this section.
  • Standardize: This is the analysis screen while performing a drift correction and is opened automatically when a batch is initiated. This screen is functionally similar to 'Quantitative Analysis'.
  • View Results: Shows the results of each sample analyzed on the machine. This window also allows you to add burns to or edit analyses.
